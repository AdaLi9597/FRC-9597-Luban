Chapter 01

Rules Analysis And Match Strategy

Kickoff Analysis

Fuel Scoring Accuracy

For a fuel to count as scored, it must fully enter the opening at the top of the hub and be processed through the system. Balls that bounce out or fail to fully enter the hub are not counted. Therefore, accuracy and consistency in shooting mechanisms are critical for maximizing scoring efficiency.

Penalty Avoidance

Penalties play a significant role in match outcomes and should be carefully avoided. Common penalties include entering protected opponent zones, interfering with robots during critical actions such as climbing, and violating human player restrictions, such as reaching beyond designated boundaries.

Minor violations, known as fouls, typically award around 5 points to the opposing alliance, while more serious violations, or technical fouls, can award approximately 15 points. Severe or repeated infractions may result in yellow or red cards.

Strategic Balance

Teams must balance scoring efficiency with defensive awareness and cooperation. High-probability scoring methods include short-range shooting into the hub and fast cycling of fuels.

Defensive strategies can include blocking opponent paths to the hub or disrupting their ability to collect fuel in the neutral zone, but defensive play must avoid protected areas.

Match Phases

A typical match has three phases: a 20-second autonomous period, a 2-minute and 20-second teleoperated period, and a final 30-second endgame. During teleop, hub activation alternates between alliances, requiring teams to adapt dynamically.

Team Coordination

Strong, high-scoring robots should focus on scoring, while partners support by supplying fuel or playing defense. Medium-level robots can balance scoring and assisting roles. Lower-scoring robots can still contribute through defense, transporting game pieces, or clearing space.

Robot Constraints

Robots must weigh no more than approximately 125 pounds, including battery and bumpers. At the start of the match, the robot must fit within the defined frame perimeter. Safety is a top priority, and all electrical and mechanical systems must follow official FRC regulations.

Chapter 02

Technical Design Notes

Mechanical Entry

Drive Train

The drive train is designed for stable movement, reliable field adaptability, and fast direction changes during defense and cycling. The layout keeps wiring accessible, supports quick repairs between matches, reduces unnecessary weight, and protects important components inside the frame.

We chose a swerve-style drive train because the robot needs to move in any direction without wasting time turning the whole chassis first. This gives the driver better control when lining up to collect game pieces, avoiding defense, or adjusting before shooting. The chassis layout also leaves a clear center area for electronics, making wiring cleaner and reducing the chance that cables interfere with moving mechanisms.

  • Easy For Wiring: electronics stay visible and reachable.
  • Quick To Repair: modules can be inspected between matches.
  • Light Weight: unnecessary plates and supports are reduced.
  • Protection: important parts are placed inside the frame area.

Mechanical Entry

Intake

The intake subsystem is optimized for reliable ground pickup and fast cycling. We tested a three-roller version, but it added friction, complexity, and more failure points. The final two-roller method is simpler, lighter, easier to tune, and keeps the pickup method easy for students to maintain.

The intake is the first part of the scoring path, so it must be fast and forgiving. A complicated intake can look stronger in CAD, but during testing we focused on whether students could adjust it quickly and whether game pieces entered the robot smoothly from different angles. The simpler design reduced tuning time and helped the team keep a steady match rhythm.

  • Fast Pace: fewer contact points help game pieces enter faster.
  • Easy Method: simpler rollers make adjustment easier.
  • Lower Risk: fewer parts means fewer possible failures.

Iteration Entry

Feeder

The feeder uses a multi-lane roller system to move game pieces efficiently toward the launcher. Earlier PC separators created tight spacing where several balls could compress and jam. Opening the feeder path lets game pieces adjust naturally, reducing compression and improving flexibility, structure strength, and maintainability.

The main engineering problem was not only moving one game piece, but moving several game pieces without forcing them into a jammed line. When four balls entered in one row, the original separators made the path too strict. Removing the tight separators gave the game pieces more space to self-correct while still being guided by the roller structure.

  • Efficient: smoother flow reduces waiting time before shooting.
  • Strong Structure: side plates and rollers stay stable under load.
  • Flexible: open space lets balls shift instead of locking together.

Iteration Entry

Column And Shooter

During testing, game pieces could get stuck near the X60 motor gap. We added a front blocker and reshaped the side PC sheets to guide pieces toward the center. This created a smoother path, reduced interruptions, increased throughput from 6 BPS to 13 BPS, and made feed rate and shooting speed more consistent.

The column and shooter are responsible for turning the feeder output into accurate scoring. Small gaps or uneven transitions can change the timing of each ball, which affects both feed rate and launch consistency. By closing the problem area near the motor and guiding balls toward a more predictable path, the shooter receives game pieces with better spacing and more stable timing.

  • Increase BPS: throughput improved from 6 BPS to 13 BPS.
  • Smooth Path: blockers prevent balls from entering dead zones.
  • Multi-Angle: software can adjust launch angle for different shots.
  • Three Separate Paths: separated flow improves organization.

Endgame Entry

Hopper And Hook

The hopper organizes game pieces before they enter the feeder using guided flow to reduce friction and keep handling consistent. The hook is designed to hang the robot during endgame with secure deployment, stable contact, and 100% accurate engagement.

The hopper supports the rest of the scoring system by preventing sudden pileups before the feeder. For endgame, the hook must be reliable because there is very little time to recover from a failed climb. The design focuses on a strong contact point, clear deployment motion, and repeatable alignment so the driver can trust the mechanism under competition pressure.

  • Guided Flow: game pieces are organized before entering the feeder.
  • Endgame Hang: the hook is built for climbing, not storage.
  • Accuracy: the target is 100% accurate engagement in endgame.

Chapter 03

Programming And Control Notes

Vision Entry

Auto-Aiming

Auto-aiming uses camera feedback, calibrated scoring parameters, and controlled launcher output to help the robot align with scoring targets. The system calculates position and angle from real-time field information, improving shooting consistency from different locations.

The goal of auto-aiming is to reduce the amount of manual correction required from drivers. The camera reads AprilTag information and helps estimate the robot's relative position to the scoring target. After the target position is understood, the program can adjust the shooting angle and prepare the launcher more consistently than a fully manual shot.

  • AprilTag Detection: vision data helps locate the target.
  • Angle Adjustment: shooter settings respond to robot position.
  • Consistency: repeated calculations reduce human aiming error.

Autonomous Entry

Autonomous And PID Control

Autonomous mode combines vision, odometry, planned arc paths, AprilTag markers, and PID control for more accurate positioning. The system corrects drift, keeps the robot aligned, supports high scoring paths, and gives the alliance wider strategic choices.

In autonomous, the robot must complete tasks without driver control, so path planning and position correction are both important. We use planned paths to define where the robot should travel, while odometry and vision help compare the planned position with the real position. PID control is used to reduce the difference between those two positions smoothly instead of making sudden movements.

This makes autonomous more reliable when the robot needs to collect game pieces, avoid alliance partners, and line up for scoring. A good autonomous routine should not only score high points, but also leave the robot in a useful location for teleop and support cooperation with alliance partners.

  • High Scoring: routines are designed to reach 50+ point potential.
  • High Cooperation: paths avoid blocking alliance partners.
  • Wide Choice: multiple routes let the team adapt to match strategy.
  • PID Positioning: smoother correction improves accuracy and stability.

Control Entry

Semi-Automatic Control

Semi-automatic control gives drivers a reliable option between full manual operation and autonomous routines. It helps operators shoot without manual control, improving efficiency and consistency and increasing the number of cycles the robot can complete during each transition.

Semi-automatic control is useful during teleop because it lets the driver team keep control of the robot while the program handles repeated precision tasks. Instead of manually tuning every movement, operators can trigger a prepared action such as aiming, adjusting shooter settings, or stabilizing a scoring sequence. This reduces pressure on the drive team and helps the robot repeat successful actions more often.

  • Operator Support: repeated actions require fewer manual steps.
  • Cycle Efficiency: faster transitions create more scoring chances.
  • Fallback Control: drivers can still use manual control when needed.

Chapter 04

Testing, Reflection, And Next Steps

Importance Of Efficiency

Work efficiency is extremely important in every task. We should arrange our steps reasonably and make good use of time. Repeated tests help us find hidden problems in advance, correct mistakes quickly, and make our work more stable and reliable.

Regional Learning

The regional event taught us the importance of reliability, simple robust systems, clear alliance communication, better match testing, and staying resilient when problems appear on the field.

Houston Goal

For Houston, our main goal is to secure a solid average overall performance. Because this is our first time taking part in this event, we prioritize learning, practical experience, observation, communication, and steady improvement.
